Peace can be defined in many ways, showing the universal aspect which exceeds cultures and religions, representing harmony and tranquility within individuals and communities. Peace isn’t simply an absence of conflicts, peace includes a variety of aspects: solidarity, justice, compassion and many more themes influencing the way peace is believed globally. Across different religious traditions, peace has its own unique meaning. Peace can be defined in more than one way. Everyone has their own unique way to define peace, outlining new aspects of guidance and ways of harmony. In Buddhism, peace and tranquility is a monumental feature to reaching the final goal of Nirvana. Peace is primarily viewed as an internal aspect, holding a significant focus on self discipline and mindfulness. Teachings like Ahimsa, Metta and Karuna help emphasise the importance of peace and removal of suffering from individuals' minds. Through pursuing inner peace, individuals are taught to reject desire and ignorance, primary causes of suffering. This focus further initiates that peace needs to be found with intent within individuals, requiring the removal of craving, attachment and ignorance, practicing the teachings of ahimsa and karuna to reach the ultimate goal of the cessation of suffering (Nirvana). Within Christianity, world peace is advocated primarily through the words of Jesus, emphasising the significance of love, reconciliation, justice and solidarity. Christian teachings encourage the adherents to move towards resolving inequality, showing justice through concepts such as liberation theology which exemplifies the taking care of whomever, including the poor and disadvantaged. In addition, the vast concepts represent the required actions to address injustices within everyday society. Through primary teachings like ‘loving thy neighbour’ Christianity promotes a strong vision on world peace. This understanding of peace is reinforced within the statement by Eleanor Roosevelt, First Lady of the United States of America 1951, “It isn’t enough to talk about peace. One must believe in it. and it isn’t enough to believe in it. One must work at it,” which portrays that peace requires a deep inner belief in conjunction with an outward effort. Ultimately, through both religious teachings, Buddhism and Christianity, it is demonstrated that peace isn’t straightforward, it is a process which requires strong commitment and actions, resulting in a transformation for the better.

Adherents of Buddhism are guided through this transformation process, towards inner peace, through the teachings within the Dhammapada, advocating for inner peace through Ahimsa and Karuna (compassion). As shown in Dhammapada 129, “All tremble at violence; All fear death. Seeing others as being like yourself. Do not kill or cause others to kill.” which teaches that all beings fear violence and death, asking adherents to use themselves as an example to avoid harming others. The extract highlights the desire upon all beings for survival, strongly promoting non-violence and compassion to be the foundation within everyday ethical conduct. Furthermore, the Metta Sutta states “Even as a mother protects with her life her child, her only child, so with a boundless heart should one cherish all living beings. Radiating kindness over the entire world, spreading upwards to the sky and downwards to the depths, outward and unbounded, free from hatred and ill will.” which reflects on the unconditional love of a mother to her children, exclaiming that this ‘motherly-love’ should be present between all beings, not only between a mother and her children. Through the exclamation upon ‘motherly-love’ the Metta Sutta promotes the inner peace teachings of ahimsa and karuna (compassion). Compassion is strongly shown within this example through requesting the treatment of others as if a mother was protecting her child. This represents a deep empathetic and concerned view for the well being of others. In addition, Dhammapada 183 states “Not to do evil, to cultivate merit, to purify one’s mind - this is the Teaching of the Buddhas”. Dhammapada 183 is the blueprint for achieving inner peace, through integrating Ahimsa and Karuna into everyday life. This scripture reference strongly shows the deep relationship between the idea of ahimsa to compassion. The compassionate want to cause no violence to any beings: animals, plants and the whole world. Through teachings shown within sacred texts like the Dhammapada or the Metta Sutta, Buddhist adherents are provided with guidance to complete the transition towards total inner peace. Through these references, Roosevelt’s quote is exemplified showing that to find peace intent is a requirement. This aspect of intent is a prominent figure within Buddhism, adherents only reaping the benefits if they complete actions with intent. Like in the Metta Sutta example, The ‘motherly-love’ must be shown with intent, or else the results like skillful karma will not be received. Intent is the foundation of Buddhism, explicitly defined by the Buddha that the intention or motivation behind an action is more significant than the action itself.

Teachings of inner peace within Buddhism are demonstrated through the words and actions of the 14th Dalai Lama. The Dalai Lama is a significant authoritative source who promotes peace through compassion and ahimsa. He states “In order to sincerely carry out ahimsa, you must respect others, which is you must develop karuna” which highlights importance of compassion to non violence, stating that karuna must be developed. This further embodies the idea that inner peace is achieved through the empathy of others, not through simply avoiding harmful actions. Additionally, the Dalai Lama states “To be happy it is our minds we must transform. This is the basis of the longstanding traditions of ahimsa, doing no harm, and karuna, wishing others to be free from suffering” which explains to adherents that peace is reliant upon an individual's inner change. This directly correlates to Roosevelt’s quote, stating that the intent for the mind's transformation is more significant than external actions when searching for peace. A contemporary example of these teachings can be through the ‘Walk for Peace’. The ‘Walk for Peace’ is a large walking meditation, organised to promote peace, loving-kindness (metta) and compassion (karuna) directly battling the current hatred and diversion of the time. Through both the teachings of the Dalai Lama and the contemporary example of the ‘Walk for Peace’ it is clearly evident that finding inner peace requires effort and intent. This strongly links to Roosevelt’s quote, showing that peace can’t be achieved only by thinking of it, it has to be achieved through personal transformation and intent.

Christianity encourages world peace through teachings of justice and solidarity, encouraging adherents to become more interconnected within one another. Justice is a primary teaching within Christianity, proclaiming the need for fairness and equality within society. This is strongly shown in Matthew 5:6, “Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they shall be filled”, which is aimed to encourage adherents to strive for justice, actively showing acts of justice, not just being a bystander. This teaching exclaims the need for fairness within the search for world peace, if injustice isn’t repented division within society can occur. Furthermore, Galatians 3:28 states, “There is no longer Jew or Greek; there is no longer slave or free; there is no longer male and female, for all of you are one Christ Jesus”, aiming to show that everyone is the same, regardless of the cultural or societal difference everyone is the same. This links to the idea of dignity within Christianity. Through ‘Imago Dei’ it is believed that everyone is created equally. Everyone is created in the image of God. Without this dignity being acknowledged, peace between individuals cannot be fulfilled. This is further represented through Liberation Theology, a concept calling Christians to support the oppressed, further showing that justice requires action to create change. Solidarity is another central theme, strongly contributing to the search for world peace through finding unity and agreement within individuals. This is evident within 1 John 3:16-18, which states that love should be shown through “in deed and truth”. This script shows how solidarity requires individuals to interact together, taking care of each other, this will then create a stronger peaceful community. This further aligns with Roosevelt’s quote, showing how world peace requires action, adherents must work together to achieve peace. World peace must be achieved through action.

Major authoritative sources like the teachings of contemporary Christian leaders like Pope Leo XIV strongly promote world peace. Pope Leo XIV states, “There can be no peace without justice. The poor remind us of this in many ways, through migration as well as through their cries, which are often stifled by the myth of well-being and progress” which describes how peace cannot be achieved within the world when inequality and injustice is present. This portrays how world peace requires effort to overcome injustice, specifically mentioning the oppression of the poor, strongly linking to liberation theology. In collaboration, Pope Leo XIV further exclaims the idea of solidarity through, “We cannot remain silent in the face of the suffering of so many defenseless people who are victims of these conflicts. What hurts them hurts all of humanity.” which shows how humans are one, humans are interconnected. This extract further links to 1 John 3:16-18, calling adherents to show love through ‘in deed and truth’ actions not only words. A contemporary example of this would be through organisations like Caritas, a global organisation working alongside the poor and oppressed communities, providing humanitarian aid to the less fortunate. Through these acts of liberation theology the organisation Caritas actively shows the peace teachings of justice and solidarity. Through this active work and action within communities, Roosevelt’s quote is clearly portrayed, showing the belief in being active within communities to achieve world peace.

In conclusion, religious traditions like Buddhism and Christianity act as significant role models in promoting both inner and world peace, showing direct and strong teachings and themes to produce harmony within individuals and communities. Buddhism shows the finding of inner peace through the peace teachings of ahimsa and karuna (compassion), guiding adherents to initiate a transformation within their minds. This search for inner peace further teaches the importance of intent. On the other hand, the search for world peace can be represented strongly within Christianity. Christianity promotes world peace through the themes of justice and solidarity. These teachings encourage adherents to combat the inequality and unfairness of the world, through aspects like liberation theology, using justice to provide care for the poor and marginalised and solidarity to connect humans together. Both religious traditions strongly represent the quote by Eleanor Roosevelt, First Lady of the United States of America 1951, which is “It isn’t enough to talk about peace. One must believe in it. and it isn’t enough to believe in it. One must work at it,” which explains that peace can’t be achieved without action. Both Buddhism and Christianity both represent this idea, either through inner or world peace, a focus on intent is needed. While focusing on separate aspects of peace, inner and world, both religious traditions promote peace through combinations of scripture, authoritative peoples and ever growing practices, all showing how peace is truly achievable.
